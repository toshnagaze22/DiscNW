From the wall · Youth · Summer camps, ages 7–18

Priya, 11 — first summer camp

First camp, first forehand.

Priya’s name went on a camp list in March. By the second morning of Summer Youth Ultimate Camp she could explain a force; by Thursday the forehand stopped wobbling and flew flat. Camps run all summer for ages 7–18 — no experience assumed, no tryout at the gate.

Nobody here asks whether a kid is “a natural.” They ask her name, and then they throw with her.

From the wall · School · Fall leagues & Spring Reign

Eun-ji, 16 — Roosevelt HS

Fall belongs to the schools.

Eun-ji plays fall high school league for Roosevelt — October games in the drizzle, spirit circles in the near-dark by season’s end. Middle school and high school leagues run every autumn across the Sound, and a school that has never fielded a team can field one by September.

Then in April her name travels to Burlington for Spring Reign, the world’s largest mixed youth ultimate tournament — more first names on one set of fields than any weekend all year.

The rule underneath every name

Ten thousand names. Zero referees.

Every DiscNW game is self-officiated, at every age, in every division. When a call is close, it gets settled the way it has been settled since 1995 — by first names, out loud, between the people playing. Spirit of the Game is not a banner slogan; it is the entire officiating system, and it holds.

“I had it, Kai.” “Good call, Eun-ji.” “Send it back, Marcus.”

From the wall · Adult · Hat league, Wonder League, Sea Plastic

Marcus, 34 — Tuesday hat league

Put your name in the hat.

Marcus moved to Seattle in February knowing one person. In June he put his name, just his name, into the Summer Mixed Hat League, which drafts strangers into teams and lets a season do the rest. By August, Tuesday was the night fourteen people yelled “MARCUS” when he laid out.

Adult ultimate here runs the calendar: hat leagues, the Summer Wonder League on the long June evenings, and Sea Plastic — the beach tournament where the whole roster takes its cleats off.

From the wall · Everyone · DiscNW Coaching Collective

Renata, 41 — first-year coach

Some names teach.

Renata came to pick her kid up from practice and left holding a clipboard. The DiscNW Coaching Collective trains the people who teach the game, school teams and club alike. So a kid’s first coach is a coached coach, and a parent’s first season on the sideline comes with a curriculum.

Ten thousand players need a few hundred sideline voices. Most of them started exactly like Renata: nearby, curious, asked once.

From the wall · Everyone · Potlatch, since 1995

Dale, 62 — still cutting deep

Some names go back to 1995.

Dale’s first Potlatch was the one that started all of this — the 1995 tournament that grew into a nonprofit. These days his name shares a holiday-weekend roster with players born three decades after his first pull. He still cuts deep. They still throw it to him.

That is the whole arc of this organization in one name: you can join at seven, and nobody ever asks you to leave.

About this direction

Nº 11 — First Names

Thesis

“An Ultimate community without barriers” is not a slogan — it is 10,000 first names. The page is a living roster: a composed census of names as the hero, five of them followed through real programs until the roster becomes stories, and one thing the whole page argues for: Add your name.

Signature

The wall of first names assembling in fast, tight waves; followed names resurfacing with an audience-colored highlight; the 10,000+ counter; and the formline mark closing the page as the one name that holds all of them.

Palette — jersey inks on warm paper

Warm Paper #FAF4E8 Roster Ink #23201B Camp Green #17703D League Blue #2C55C4 Hat-League Brick #B23A1C

Type

Bricolage Grotesque — a warm humanist sans with real personality; the names wall is the typography specimen. Instrument Sans — quiet body, captions, and details.

Motion

Census, not confetti: the names rise like a crowd arriving, the photos wipe in, the counter rolls once, and the five followed names light up when they come back. Everything settles within 2.3 seconds. Turn motion off, and the roster is already there.

Choose this if

You want the mission self-evident: people first, each program shown as what it does for a person. And you want a hero built from what you already have — ten thousand real names.

Think twice if

You need program-first wayfinding on day one, or invented sample names would sit uneasily beside your real member data. This direction is strongest when the wall eventually goes real — opt-in first names from actual registration.
