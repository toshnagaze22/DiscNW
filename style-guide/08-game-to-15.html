Fifteen points. Fifteen true things about ultimate in the Northwest. DiscNW pulls; you receive. Every point you read is a point you score.

Press ↓ to pull

Point 01 · The vision0 – 1
01

An Ultimate community without barriers.

That is DiscNW’s entire vision, in six words. Nobody gets stopped at the field, and the mission carries no fine print. The next fourteen points are what those six words cost, and what they buy.

Point 02 · The scale0 – 2
02

Ten thousand players. And counting.

More than 10,000 people play with DiscNW every year, from Magnuson Park to grass fields all around Puget Sound. It adds up to a small city that sprints.

Point 03 · The rules0 – 3
03

Zero referees. Since the first pull.

DiscNW games are self-officiated: players make the calls and settle the disputes themselves. That system is called Spirit of the Game, and thirty seasons in it still works.

Point 04 · The origin0 – 4
04

Born at Potlatch, 1995.

A tournament needed an organization, so the players built one. DiscNW grew out of the Potlatch tournament in 1995 and has been organizing the region’s ultimate ever since.

Point 05 · The span0 – 5
05

Ages 7 to 70-plus.

Summer Youth Ultimate Camps start at seven, and masters hat leagues never really stop. The disc and the field lines stay the same across a playing lifetime.

Point 06 · The flagship0 – 6
06

The largest mixed youth ultimate tournament in the world.

It is called Spring Reign, and it happens here every spring. Nothing in mixed youth ultimate, anywhere on earth, is bigger. Middle schoolers headline it.

Point 07 · The fee0 – 7
07

From $33. Sliding scale. No questions.

Registration slides down to $33 so that money never keeps anyone off the field. You pay what works for you, and nobody checks. Barrier-free here is a line item in the budget.

Point 09 · The season0 – 9
09

Fall belongs to the schools.

The Middle School and High School leagues turn hallways into rosters across the region each autumn. For a lot of kids, sixth grade is where the first pull happens.

Point 10 · The hotline0 – 10
10

(206)
781‑5840

The rainout hotline. Seattle ultimate was checking field closures by phone long before apps and push alerts, and on a wet morning this number still answers.

Point 11 · The beach0 – 11
11

One weekend a year is played on sand.

Sea Plastic trades the grass for sand and the drizzle for salt air. It is the one weekend a year when players get to leave their cleats at home.

Point 12 · The coaches0 – 12
12

Coaches, coached.

The DiscNW Coaching Collective trains the people who teach the game, school team and club alike, so a kid’s first coach has been coached too.

Point 13 · The engine0 – 13
13

Run on volunteers.

Field-liners, score reporters, tournament directors, board members. The organization runs on people who show up early, stay late, and take a shift because somebody once took one for them.

Point 14 · The roster spot0 – 14
14

You.

Fourteen points in, one thing missing. Look at the scoreboard and notice who has scored every point on this page. That is the model: DiscNW pulls. You score.

About this direction

07 · Game to 15

The site is a night game played to 15, under the lights. A real LED scoreboard scores your reading as fifteen glowing dot-matrix numerals carry you from the first pull to a full-screen game point. A chalk field HUD tracks the disc up the field with every point, a pull arcs across each numeral as it lands, and a halftime highlight loop breaks in. Stadium scoreboard on the outside, storytelling on the inside.

Palette
Night Field #10281A Chalk #F4F2E6 Universe Orange #FF4A00 Amber LED #FFB61E Tidal LED #3FC1D4
Type
Doto, a dot-matrix face, for every scoreboard digit and HUD readout. Space Grotesk for the chrome. Newsreader for the reading columns, chalk on the night ground.
Signature
The LED board: “DISCNW 0 – 0 YOU” in glowing dot-matrix with a blinking live dot and a fifteen-block momentum bar that fills as you read. Every numeral is a scoreboard digit in its own color, a chalk field diagram walks the disc to the end zone, and at game point the whole stadium floods orange.
Choose this if
You want the page people retell in one sentence: “the site is a night game to 15.” The scoreboard language is drawn straight from how the sport keeps score, and the video-game energy meets kids and players where they are.
Think twice if
You need a working homepage, not a manifesto: schedules, standings, and registration paths still require one of the grounded directions underneath this voice.
