Sideline Day

The truest picture of DiscNW isn’t a highlight reel — it’s a Saturday at the fields: camp chairs, orange slices, a first layout, a spirit circle. The site should feel like being welcomed onto that sideline.

  • Sunny
  • Welcoming
  • Candid
  • Plainspoken
  • Legible
  • Reassuring

01 · The idea

Design the site for the family who found you last night

Most sports sites talk to people who already play. This direction talks to the family googling “ultimate frisbee Seattle kids” on a Tuesday night — and to the adult who hasn’t played anything since college. It looks like the thing itself: a bright Saturday at the fields, in full daylight, with nothing to decode.

Your mission says “an Ultimate community without barriers.” Here, that’s a design decision: big readable type, plain words, sliding-scale fees up front, and photographs left exactly as the day looked. A small family of sticker badges answers a nervous parent’s questions, like “ages 7–18” and “no experience needed,” before they even scroll.

02 · Color

Full daylight on a white page

Grass, sky, and sun — the colors already on the field every Saturday. They live on buttons, badges, and panels; the photographs stay untouched, because the daylight in them is the palette. Nothing is pastel, and the ink is deep evergreen rather than black.

04 · Signature element

The water-bottle sticker system

Badges styled like the vinyl on a player’s water bottle: same die-cut white edge, same evergreen outline, same soft shadow. They’re curated like a good sticker sheet, so this entire page wears exactly seven. One answers a question, one is the club’s own mark, one is real event art, untouched.

  1. One per view. A sticker earns its corner or waits for the next page. Seven on this entire page — count them.
  2. Three angles only. −3°, 2°, −2°. Like a careful hand placed them, and the crest always signs at 2°. Nothing tumbles, nothing spins.
  3. Never on a face, never under text. Stickers keep to photo corners and page margins; people and words always win.

Before the die-cut edge, it’s just program art. These are real DiscNW program badges, shown flat and untouched on a plain tile — the same art a sticker is cut from, like the Sea Plastic duck up on the sheet.

09 · Voice & tone

How this direction talks

Plain verbs, second person, zero jargon until we’ve taught the word first. It sounds like the friendliest parent on the sideline — the one who waves you over and lends you a chair.

Hero headline

Come play. We’ll teach you everything.

Registration confirmation

“You’re in! Maya is registered for Summer Youth Ultimate Camps at Magnuson Park, 9am–3pm. We’ve emailed the details — pack cleats, water, lunch, and a warm layer for Seattle mornings.”

To a nervous first-time parent

“You don’t need to know a single rule yet. Most of our parents learned the game from a camp chair on the sideline, and the kids love explaining it. Bring a chair — we’ll handle the rest.”

Is Sideline Day the right call?

Choose this direction if…

Growing the community is the job — you want the site that recruits new families and first-time players hardest, and makes “no barriers” something a visitor can feel in the first five seconds.

Think twice if…

Your competitive club players and tournament veterans need to see themselves first — this direction leads with newcomers, and elite intensity rides in the back seat.
