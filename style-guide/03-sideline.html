Sideline Day

The truest picture of DiscNW isn’t a highlight reel — it’s a Saturday at the fields: camp chairs, orange slices, a first layout, a spirit circle. The site should feel like being welcomed onto that sideline.

  • Sunny
  • Welcoming
  • Candid
  • Plainspoken
  • Legible
  • Reassuring

01 · The idea

Design the site for the family who found you last night

Most sports sites talk to people who already play. This direction talks to the family googling “ultimate frisbee Seattle kids” on a Tuesday night — and to the adult who hasn’t played anything since college. It looks like the thing itself: a bright Saturday at the fields, in full daylight, with nothing to decode.

Your mission says “an Ultimate community without barriers.” Here, that’s a design decision: big readable type, plain words, sliding-scale fees up front, and a sticker-badge system that answers a nervous parent’s questions (“ages 7–18”, “no experience needed”) before they even scroll. The competitive programs are all still here — they just stop being the front door.

02 · Color

Full daylight on a white page

Grass, sky, and sun — the colors already on the field every Saturday. Everything sits on crisp white so photos of real kids and real weather do the talking. Friendly, but calibrated for teenagers and adult rec players too: the ink is deep evergreen, not black, and nothing is pastel.

03 · Typography

Rounded where it welcomes, rigorous where it informs

One size up from typical everywhere. This site gets read on phones, at arm’s length, on a sunny sideline — clarity beats flair.

Designed by the Braille Institute for maximum legibility — a body face that takes “a community without barriers” literally.

Ultimate is a non-contact sport played with a flying disc and officiated by the players themselves — even the 12-year-olds. Games run rain or shine (this is Seattle), and every DiscNW program uses sliding-scale fees, so the price fits your household — not the other way around.

04 · Signature element

The water-bottle sticker system

Program and age-group badges styled like the vinyl stickers on a player’s water bottle — slightly rotated, die-cut white edges, bold evergreen outlines — plus a hand-drawn disc-flight doodle that underlines headlines and carries the eye between sections.

Ages 7–18 Free pickup · year-round Hat league = instant team

Stickers tag every program card with the answers newcomers actually need — age range, experience level, format — so reassurance travels with the content instead of living on a buried FAQ page. Existing event art already speaks this language: the duck is DiscNW’s real Sea Plastic tournament badge, dropped onto the sheet untouched.

…and every big headline gets a throw

The disc-flight doodle: a hand-drawn underline and section divider, drawn once, used everywhere. It’s the site’s handwriting.

09 · Voice & tone

How this direction talks

Plain verbs, second person, zero jargon until we’ve taught the word first. It sounds like the friendliest parent on the sideline — the one who waves you over and lends you a chair.

Hero headline

Come play. We’ll teach you everything.

Registration confirmation

“You’re in! Maya is registered for Summer Youth Ultimate Camps at Magnuson Park, 9am–3pm. We’ve emailed the details — pack cleats, water, lunch, and a warm layer for Seattle mornings.”

To a nervous first-time parent

“You don’t need to know a single rule yet. Most of our parents learned the game from a camp chair on the sideline, and the kids love explaining it. Bring a chair — we’ll handle the rest.”

Is Sideline Day the right call?

Choose this direction if…

Growing the community is the job — you want the site that recruits new families and first-time players hardest, and makes “no barriers” something a visitor can feel in the first five seconds.

Think twice if…

Your competitive club players and tournament veterans need to see themselves first — this direction leads with newcomers, and elite intensity deliberately rides in the back seat.
